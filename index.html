The Bind/Surest Model: What If We Go This Direction?

Bind (now Surest/UHC) proved that eliminating deductibles in favor of variable, transparent copays creates better outcomes for both employers and members. Here's what it would mean for our build.

The Core Insight

Deductibles don't work. They frustrate members, they don't drive informed shopping behavior, and they create a "donut hole" where members avoid care early in the year. Bind's thesis: replace the entire deductible/coinsurance structure with variable copays that differ by provider and service — so members see one transparent price before they get care, and that price incentivizes them toward higher-value providers.

The "Add-In" Coverage Innovation

Bind's most novel feature: for ~30 plannable procedures (knee surgery, cataract removal, back surgery, etc.), base coverage doesn't include them. Members "activate" coverage when they need it, paying an additional premium spread across future paychecks. This is like on-demand insurance for big-ticket procedures.

Key Design Decisions For Our Build

If we adopt the Bind model, here are the critical architectural and strategic choices we'd need to make.

Variable Copay Pricing Engine

How do we set provider-specific copays?

Bind/Surest uses a proprietary algorithm that scores providers on quality, efficiency, and cost, then maps scores to copay tiers. Lower copay = higher-value provider. This requires: claims data for benchmarking, a provider scoring model, and actuarial validation that the copay structure is revenue-neutral for the plan.

Build decision: This is a core AI competency. Our scoring model + the plan configuration compiler can generate provider-level copays from network rate data and quality metrics. This is where our AI advantage is strongest.

Actuarial Equivalence

How do we make $0 deductible cost-neutral for the employer?

Removing the deductible shifts costs to the plan. You offset this with: higher copays on low-value providers (incentivizing steerage), the add-in premium structure for plannable procedures, and behavioral savings from members choosing lower-cost settings. Surest reports 5–15% total cost savings vs. traditional plans — but proving this actuarially for your first client is critical.

Build decision: You need a credentialed actuary (FSA) to validate the plan design. This is not something AI can replace — regulators and stop-loss carriers require human actuarial sign-off. Engage an actuarial consulting firm (Milliman, Oliver Wyman, or a boutique) early.

Add-In Premium Mechanics

Do we implement the on-demand add-in model?

The add-in model is innovative but adds complexity: you need payroll integration to adjust premiums mid-period, actuarial pricing for each add-in procedure, an activation workflow with a waiting period (to prevent adverse selection), and ACA compliance review (the plan must still meet minimum value requirements).

Recommendation: Start without add-ins for V1. Launch with the variable copay / no-deductible model first. Add the on-demand layer in V2 once you have claims data to price it actuarially and payroll integrations to support dynamic premiums.

490+ Service Pricing

How do we price every service transparently?

Surest shows upfront prices for 490+ services. This requires: mapping every CPT/HCPCS code to a member-facing service description, calculating member cost for each service at each provider (using network rates + copay algorithm), and bundling related services (e.g., a surgery copay includes surgeon, anesthesia, facility, and follow-up).

Build decision: This is the hardest AI/data problem in the Bind model. Start with the top 100 most common services (which cover ~80% of member interactions) and expand. The service bundling logic is deeply complex and is exactly where your domain expert hire is critical.

Provider Steerage & Legal Risk

Can we steer members without legal exposure?

Variable copays inherently steer members toward certain providers. This is legal under ERISA for self-insured plans, but raises considerations: any willing provider laws in some states (though ERISA preempts most for self-insured), provider relations (hospitals may push back against being labeled "high copay"), and member perception (are we restricting choice or empowering it?).

Framing matters: "We show you the price so you can decide" is very different from "We penalize you for choosing this doctor." The Bind model succeeds because it's positioned as transparency and choice, not restriction. Your AI-powered member experience must reinforce this framing.

Stop-Loss Compatibility

Will stop-loss carriers accept a non-traditional plan design?

Stop-loss carriers are conservative. A $0-deductible plan with variable copays is unfamiliar to most underwriters. They may charge higher premiums or decline to quote. You need to demonstrate: actuarial soundness, that total plan cost is comparable or lower than traditional designs, and that your adjudication engine correctly applies the variable copay structure.

Start the stop-loss conversation early, with the actuarial analysis in hand. Sun Life and Voya have experience with innovative plan designs. Having a credentialed actuary present the design to the stop-loss carrier is essential.

Strategic Assessment

The Bind model is a compelling direction with proven results (5–15% employer savings, 44% lower member OOP, 90% re-enrollment). It plays directly to AI strengths: the variable copay pricing engine, real-time member price lookup, and provider quality scoring are all AI-native capabilities.

However, it raises the bar on three fronts: actuarial complexity (you must prove the $0-deductible design is cost-equivalent or better), provider data requirements (you need cost and quality data at the provider level for every service), and stop-loss acceptance (carriers need to be comfortable with a non-traditional design).

Recommended approach: Design the system architecture to support variable copays from day one, but launch your first clients on a simplified version — $0 deductible, tiered copays (3–4 tiers based on provider value), no add-ins. This gets 80% of the Bind benefit with 40% of the complexity. Then evolve toward full provider-level variable pricing and add-ins as your data and actuarial confidence grow.

Regulatory Considerations

Critical licensing and compliance requirements for an AI-native health plan.

🏛

TPA Licensing

Most states require TPA licensure. Requirements include financial solvency, state filing, and agreement to examination. This is a necessary early step.

⚖

ERISA Fiduciary Risk

A TPA exercising discretionary control may be deemed a fiduciary by courts, carrying significant liability. ERISA counsel is essential from day one.

🤖

AI-Specific Regulation

CMS requires clinical judgment in coverage decisions. States are introducing human-review requirements for AI denials. Explainability requirements are increasing.

🔒

HIPAA & Data Security

Processing massive PHI volumes requires strong HIPAA compliance, BAAs, SOC 2 Type II certification, and careful handling of AI model training on health data.

Key Takeaways

Six strategic conclusions for building an AI-native health plan.

Massive, dissatisfied market

67% of covered workers are in self-funded plans run on legacy systems. Employers want transparency and better service.

AI can automate 60–70% of operations

Claims, prior auth, member services, eligibility, and analytics are highly automatable — translating to lower costs and faster service.

Humans essential for judgment & relationships

Provider contracting, complex clinical decisions, legal compliance, and stop-loss placement require experienced professionals.

Network access is the hardest problem

Rent first, build direct contracts over time. No amount of AI solves the cold-start network problem.

No one has built this yet

Existing AI startups attack individual functions. The full-stack, unified platform opportunity remains wide open.

Regulatory tailwinds with careful navigation

CMS interoperability rules and PA mandates favor tech-forward entrants, but AI regulation is evolving fast.
